I also realize how silly it was to post a bunch of links that I hadn't yet read in [my last post](../links-1). Now that I've read them all, my favorites were both by Matt Lakeman. Don't let [Notes on Afghanistan](https://mattlakeman.org/2026/01/05/notes-on-afghanistan/)'s length dissuade you from reading it. It's fascinating and I learned so much about the Taliban and someone who puts an outrageous amount of effort into something stupid and dangerous. I would also recommend [Infinite Jest Extraction](https://mattlakeman.org/2026/03/26/infinite-jest-extraction/) whether you've read the book or never plan to. It wouldn't totally ruin the book if you read it first, but it's still better to go in blind. I also like it because it comports with my own [review](../infinite-jest) but actually has details about the main characters and plots, and as I predicted, I had forgotten a lot about those already.

Honorable mention goes to Dynomight's [So Much Blood](https://dynomight.net/blood/) which is short, weird, and tickles the part of my brain that likes to know where numbers come from. Also Bret Devereaux's [How your History gets made](https://acoup.blog/2020/07/09/collections-how-your-history-gets-made/) which does the same for history in general. Nothing about it was groundbreaking, but it's stuck with me because I find it trippy to think about how and what information gets filtered down from experts to the general public.
